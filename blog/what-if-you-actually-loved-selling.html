Confidence

What If You Actually
Loved Selling?

The women who struggle most with sales aren't bad at business. They're carrying a story that was never theirs to begin with.

By Liz Lemarchand · June 30, 2026 · 9 min read

Most women who come to me say some version of the same thing.

"I just hate selling."

They say it like it's a personality trait. Like it's fixed. Like some people are born knowing how to talk about their work with confidence, and they just... weren't one of them.

But here's what I've noticed after coaching women through hundreds of thousands of dollars in closed sales: the ones who say they hate selling don't actually hate selling. They hate the feeling that comes with it. The desperation. The pushiness. The sense that they're imposing, or begging, or somehow diminishing themselves just by asking someone to pay them.

That feeling has a name. And it isn't "bad at sales." It's a nervous system response to a very old story about what it means for a woman to want something, ask for something, and believe she deserves to receive it.

When you understand that, everything changes.

✦

You Were Never Taught to Sell. You Were Taught to Wait.

Think about how most women are raised around money and ambition.

Be helpful. Be likable. Don't take up too much space. Don't seem too eager. Don't ask for too much.

And then we step into business and wonder why asking someone to pay us feels like a violation.

It isn't a strategy problem. It isn't a script problem. It's a conditioning problem, rooted in the belief that wanting to be paid well for your work makes you greedy, aggressive, or somehow less of a "good" person.

Women solopreneurs carry this into every sales conversation. They over-explain. They apologize for their prices. They wait for the other person to bring up the offer because initiating feels too forward. They discount before anyone even pushes back.

None of that is a sales failure. It's a self-worth pattern playing out in real time.

✦

What Selling Actually Is (When You Strip Away the Noise)

Here's the reframe that changes things for most of my clients.

Selling is not convincing. Selling is not performing. Selling is not manipulation dressed up in strategy.

Selling, at its core, is communication. It's being clear enough about what you do and who it's for that the right person can say yes.

That's it.

When you're selling with compassion instead of pressure, you're not trying to talk someone into something. You're holding space for them to recognize themselves in what you're offering. You're making it easy for the person who already wants what you have to find their way to you.

Women who hate sales are often the best at this kind of selling, once they get out of their own way. Because they're naturally empathetic. They listen well. They care about the people they work with. They aren't interested in closing someone who isn't a real fit.

That's not a liability. That's an asset.

✦

The Side Gig That Stays Small

One of the most common patterns I see with women building a side gig to replace their 9 to 5 is this: they build everything except the sales muscle.

They create the offer. They design the brand. They post on social media. They show up. They do everything right, except they never quite get comfortable with the part where money actually changes hands.

So the side gig stays small. Not because the offer isn't good. Not because there's no market. But because somewhere between "I have something valuable" and "let me tell you what it costs," something shuts down.

That shutdown is the work. Not the funnel. Not the content calendar. Not the pricing formula.

The real question isn't "how do I find clients?" The real question is: what happens in your body the moment you think about asking someone to pay you?

If the answer is tightness, dread, a sudden urge to offer a discount, or the feeling that you're being too much, you're not dealing with a sales problem. You're dealing with a nervous system response that's been running the show for years.

✦

What Changes When the Story Changes

"I used to end every sales conversation by apologizing for my prices. Now I hold them with confidence. Nothing changed except what I believed I was worth."

That's a real shift. And it isn't about affirmations or mindset hacks or forcing yourself to feel confident before you're ready.

It's about understanding where the original story came from, and making a conscious decision that it no longer gets to run your business.

When women do this work, something unexpected tends to happen. Selling stops feeling like the thing they dread. It starts feeling like the most powerful expression of what they do.

Because when you're fully behind your offer, when you believe in the transformation you create, when you've stopped apologizing for taking up space, a sales conversation becomes something else entirely. It becomes an act of service. It becomes the moment where someone's life actually has the chance to change, because you were willing to say: here's what I do, here's what it costs, and you deserve to have this.

That is a completely different experience than begging.

✦

How to Start Shifting Your Sales Energy

You don't have to overhaul everything overnight. Here's where to begin.

Notice the story before the conversation. Before your next sales call or DM or discovery conversation, pay attention to what you're telling yourself. Are you going in with dread? Bracing for rejection? Already planning the discount? That story is the data.

Separate the feeling from the fact. The feeling that you're being pushy is not evidence that you are being pushy. Most women who worry about being too much are actually being too little. They're underselling, undercharging, and underestimating how much value they actually bring.

Get your nervous system on board. Confidence in selling isn't built through better scripts. It's built through repetition, regulation, and genuine belief in the thing you're selling. Take a breath before you state your price. Slow down. Let the offer land without rushing to fill the silence with a discount.

Sell from service, not from scarcity. When you approach a sales conversation from a place of "I genuinely believe this person needs what I offer," the energy shifts. You stop performing and start connecting. That's where real selling happens.

Build the community around you. One of the fastest ways to shift your relationship with money and selling is to be in a room with women who are doing the same. Who are honest about the fear, the conditioning, the moments they wanted to hide, and who kept going anyway.

✦

You Don't Have to Dread This Forever

Selling for women who hate sales doesn't start with a better closing technique. It starts with a different belief about what you deserve.

You built something real. You have something valuable to offer. The people who need what you do are out there, right now, looking for someone exactly like you.

The only thing standing between you and them is your willingness to say: I'm here, this is what I do, and here's how to work with me.

That's not pushy. That's brave.
