Confidence

Are You Asking for Permission
to Take Up Space?

Most women don't know they're doing it. But the language patterns are there, hidden inside every bio, email, and sales message they write.

By Liz Lemarchand · June 30, 2026 · 8 min read

There's a moment most women experience when they sit down to write about themselves or their work.

They open a blank page. They start typing. And somewhere between the first sentence and the last, something quietly happens. The language softens. The certainty disappears. Words like "just" and "maybe" and "I think" start showing up. A price becomes "only." An offer becomes "something that might help."

By the time they're done, the message is polished, warm, and completely stripped of its power.

This isn't bad writing. It's permission-seeking. And it's one of the most common invisible blocks keeping talented women from building the business or side gig they actually want.

✦

What Are Permission Signals?

Permission signals are the small, often unconscious language patterns that reveal you're asking for approval to take up space rather than simply... taking it.

They show up everywhere.

In your bio: "I just help women with..." instead of "I help women..."

In your sales message: "This might not be for everyone, but..." instead of leading with who it IS for.

In your pricing: "It's only $200" instead of "$200."

In your email subject lines, your Instagram captions, your discovery call openers. Everywhere you communicate about yourself or your work, permission signals can be quietly undermining the impact of what you're saying.

The frustrating part is that they're almost impossible to see in your own writing. Because they don't feel like weakness when you're writing them. They feel like humility. Like being considerate. Like not wanting to come across as too much.

That's what makes them so sticky.

✦

Where Permission Signals Come From

This isn't a writing problem. It's a conditioning problem.

Women are socialized from a young age to soften their presence, hedge their opinions, and make others comfortable with their ambition. We learn that confidence can read as arrogance, that directness can read as aggression, that wanting to be paid well can read as greediness.

So we pre-emptively apologize. We qualify everything. We shrink our language before anyone even pushes back, because somewhere in the nervous system, there's an old story running that says: if you take up too much space, you'll be punished for it.

For women solopreneurs especially, this plays out in real financial consequences. The bio that doesn't convert. The sales page that gets traffic but no signups. The email that gets ignored. The discovery call that ends with "I'll think about it."

It's not that the offer isn't good. It's that the language surrounding the offer is quietly signaling: I'm not sure you should trust me yet.

✦

The Invisible Tax on Your Business

Think about the last piece of content you wrote about your work. Your about page, a sales email, a social post introducing your offer.

Now read it again, looking for these patterns:

  • Minimizing words: just, only, a little, kind of, sort of, maybe
  • Unnecessary qualifiers: I think, I believe, I feel like, in my opinion
  • Pre-emptive apologies: this might not be for everyone, I know this is a lot, I hope this makes sense
  • Softened pricing: only $X, just $X, starting at $X (when there's no range)
  • Vague value claims: I help people feel better, I support women in their journey

Each one of these is a small withdrawal from the trust account you're trying to build with your reader. Individually, they seem harmless. Together, they create a cumulative signal that says: I'm not fully sure I belong here.

And your reader feels that. Even if they can't name it.

✦

What Happens When You Remove Them

"I rewrote my bio without any permission language and booked two discovery calls the same week. Nothing else changed."

That's the shift. It's not magic, and it's not manipulation. It's clarity.

When you strip out the hedging and the apologizing and the softening, what's left is the actual truth of what you do and who you are. And that truth, stated plainly, is far more compelling than any amount of careful positioning.

Confident language doesn't mean aggressive language. It doesn't mean cold or clinical or salesy. It means you trust yourself enough to say what you mean without asking for permission first.

That's what selling with compassion actually looks like. Not pressure. Not performance. Just clarity.

✦

How to Start Catching Your Own Permission Signals

The first step is awareness. You can't edit what you can't see.

Start by auditing one piece of writing — your bio, your about page, or a recent sales message. Read it out loud. Notice where your voice drops. Notice where you added a qualifier that wasn't necessary. Notice where you softened a claim that was actually true and didn't need softening.

Then rewrite just that section without the permission language. Keep the warmth. Keep the personality. Just remove the apology.

The difference will surprise you.

A few swaps to start with:

  • "I just help women with..." becomes "I help women with..."
  • "This might be helpful if..." becomes "This is for you if..."
  • "It's only $300" becomes "$300"
  • "I think what makes me different is..." becomes "What makes me different is..."

Small shifts. Real impact.

You Don't Need More Confidence to Write Confidently

That's the counterintuitive truth here.

You don't have to wait until you feel sure of yourself to write like you are. You can start by simply removing the language that signals you aren't. The feeling follows the action, not the other way around.

Your work is real. Your value is real. The people who need what you offer deserve to find you, and they can only do that if your words are clear enough to reach them.

Stop asking for permission. Start making the offer.
