How to Price Your Services
When You're Just
Starting Out

The pricing problem is almost never about numbers. Here's what's really going on — and how to charge what you're worth.

By Liz Lemarchand · June 26, 2025 · 8 min read

You've been sitting with a number in your head. Maybe for weeks. Maybe for months. It's the price you actually want to charge — the one that feels right when you're feeling good about yourself, the one that reflects your time and expertise and the real transformation you offer.

And then you go to type it on your website, or say it out loud on a call, and something happens. The number shrinks. You add a caveat. You offer a discount before they've even asked. You tell yourself you'll charge more once you have more experience, more testimonials, more proof that you're worth it.

I want to tell you something clearly: the problem is not the number. The problem is what the number means to you.

✦

Why Pricing Feels So Personal

When you put a price on your services, you're not just assigning a dollar amount to a deliverable. You're making a public statement about your worth. And for most women — especially those just starting out — that act surfaces every unresolved belief about whether they deserve to be paid well, whether they're "ready," and whether anyone will actually say yes.

Pricing triggers the same vulnerability as making an offer, asking for a raise, or standing up in a room and saying "I'm an expert at this." It requires you to claim something. And claiming things — especially claiming value — can feel dangerous when you've been conditioned to minimize yourself.

The thoughts that show up around pricing

  • "I'm just starting out — I can't charge that much yet."
  • "What if they say it's too expensive and I lose them?"
  • "I should build a portfolio first, then raise my rates."
  • "I feel guilty charging that much for something I enjoy."
  • "Other people charge less — who am I to charge more?"
  • "What if they think I'm overestimating my value?"

These aren't pricing problems. They're self-worth problems wearing the costume of a spreadsheet. And no pricing formula will solve them — only the inner work will.

That said: practical clarity matters too. So let's work through both.

✦

The Inner Work: Untangling Worth from Price

The first thing I want you to do is separate two things that have become fused in your mind: your price and your worthiness as a human being.

Your price is a business decision. It's based on the value you deliver, the market you serve, the time required, and the positioning you want. It is not a referendum on whether you deserve to exist, to be loved, or to take up space. The moment someone doesn't buy at your price, it does not mean you were wrong to charge it.

But I know that knowing this intellectually and actually feeling it in your body are two very different things. So here's a practice that helps:

The "what changes for them" exercise

Before you set your price, write out — in specific, concrete terms — what changes for a client after they work with you. Not what you do for them. What changes for them. What becomes possible. What burden lifts. What they stop losing or start gaining.

When you're clear on the transformation, pricing becomes less about defending your hourly rate and more about putting a number on an outcome. And outcomes — real, life-changing outcomes — are worth a lot more than most new entrepreneurs charge for them.

"You are not pricing your time. You are pricing what changes for someone because of the time they spent with you. Those are very different things."

The Practical Work: How to Actually Set Your Price

Once you've done the inner work — or at least started it — here's a practical framework for arriving at a number you can actually stand behind.

Start with outcomes, not hours

If you price by the hour, you're positioning yourself as a commodity. Every client can do the math and ask: "Is this person worth X per hour compared to the alternative?" Instead, package your work around results. A "six-week coaching program to help you land your first three clients" is not the same as "six hours of coaching." Price the former accordingly.

Research what the market bears — then make a conscious choice

Look at what others in your field charge. Not to copy them, but to understand the landscape. Then make a deliberate decision: do you want to compete on price (risky, exhausting, usually a race to the bottom) or on positioning and transformation (harder to establish, but far more sustainable)?

For most coaches and service providers just starting out, I recommend pricing in the mid-range for your niche — not the cheapest, not the most premium — and being crystal clear on the specific outcome you deliver. That combination is the most approachable and credible starting point.

Test your price with your body, not just your mind

Say your price out loud. In a mirror. On a voice note. Listen for the apology in your voice, the hedge, the upward inflection that turns a statement into a question. A price you believe in sounds like a statement. A price you're afraid of sounds like a question.

If it still sounds like a question after practice, you may need to go back to the inner work — or adjust the price until it feels like yours to own.

✦

The "Not Ready Yet" Trap

I want to address this specifically because it keeps so many talented women stuck for years: the belief that you need more credentials, more testimonials, more experience before you can charge what you actually want to charge.

Here's the truth. There will always be a reason to wait. Another certification you could get. Another free client you could take on to "build your portfolio." Another milestone that, once reached, will finally make you feel legitimate.

But legitimacy doesn't come from accumulation. It comes from decision. At some point, you have to decide you are ready — not because all the evidence is in, but because you've chosen to trust yourself. Your clients don't need you to be perfect. They need you to be present, capable, and clear about the value you bring. You already are all three of those things.

"The price you set is a declaration. It tells the market how you see yourself — and the market tends to believe you."

What to Do When Someone Says You're Too Expensive

It will happen. And it will sting the first few times. Here's what I want you to remember: "that's too expensive for me right now" is not the same as "you are not worth that." It means this particular person, at this particular moment, is not your client. That's information, not rejection.

The women who are meant to work with you — at your actual price — will not flinch. They will feel the value before you've even finished the sentence. Those are your people. Set your price to attract them, not to avoid the ones who won't invest.

What to remember when setting your price

  • Price the transformation, not the hours.
  • Mid-range plus crystal-clear outcome is the strongest starting position.
  • Practice saying your price until it sounds like a statement, not an apology.
  • "Not ready yet" is almost always a feeling, not a fact.
  • The right clients will not negotiate your worth — they'll recognize it.
  • You can always raise your price. Start somewhere real and move from there.
✦

Pricing is one of the most intimate acts in business because it asks you to stand in front of another person and say: this is what my work is worth. Not what I hope it's worth. Not what I'll settle for. What it's worth.

That takes practice. It takes inner work. And it takes the willingness to be uncomfortable — repeatedly — until the discomfort becomes data instead of a stop sign.

You are worth more than you're currently charging. Start there, and work forward.
