How to Find Your First Client
as an Introvert
(Without Feeling Pushy)

You don't need to be louder. You need to be more rooted — and these three shifts will change how clients find you.

By Liz Lemarchand · June 26, 2025 · 8 min read

Let me guess. You've been told — directly or indirectly — that getting clients requires you to be more visible, more vocal, more persistent. That you need to post every day, pitch constantly, and "put yourself out there" in ways that make your stomach hurt just thinking about them.

And so you haven't. Or you've tried, felt mortified, and pulled back. And now the first client feels even further away than when you started.

Here's what I want you to hear: the extroverted, high-volume approach to finding clients is one approach. It's not the only one. It's not even the best one for everyone. And if it's not working for you, that's not a character flaw — it's a signal that you need a different strategy.

Introverts make extraordinary coaches, consultants, service providers, and entrepreneurs. Not in spite of their nature, but because of it. The depth of attention, the quality of listening, the ability to hold space without needing to fill it — these are gifts. And clients feel them.

The problem isn't who you are. The problem is that you've been trying to find clients using a playbook written for someone else.

✦

First: What's Actually Getting in the Way

Before we talk strategy, let's be honest about what's really happening. Because for most introverted entrepreneurs, the block isn't actually about being introverted. It's about the story running underneath.

The thoughts that keep showing up

  • "I don't want to bother anyone."
  • "What if they think I'm only reaching out because I want something?"
  • "I'll wait until I'm more established before I start telling people."
  • "I don't have a big enough following to put myself out there."
  • "What if I reach out and they say no — and then it's awkward?"
  • "I'm not good at small talk. Networking events feel like torture."

Notice something? None of those are really about introversion. They're about fear. Fear of rejection, fear of judgment, fear of being perceived as using people. Introversion might shape how those fears show up — but the fears themselves are the work.

That said: strategy matters too. So let's talk about what actually works when you're wired the way you are.

✦

Shift One: Stop Networking. Start Connecting.

"Networking" is a word that makes most introverts want to lie down. It conjures images of conferences, small talk, and handing business cards to strangers while pretending to be fascinated by their LinkedIn profile.

Forget all of that.

What you're actually looking for is connection — genuine, one-to-one conversation with people who are already in your world. Former colleagues. People you've worked with or studied alongside. Friends who know what you're building. People you follow online whose work you genuinely admire.

Introverts are often exceptional at deep, one-to-one connection. That's not a liability — it's your superpower. And it turns out that most first clients don't come from a big audience or a viral post. They come from someone who already knows you, or someone who was referred by someone who does.

"You don't need a thousand followers. You need ten people who genuinely understand what you do — and trust that you do it well."

So instead of "networking," try this: make a list of twenty people you already have a real relationship with. Not to pitch them. Just to reconnect, share what you're building, and ask if they know anyone who might benefit. That's it. No script. No pressure. Just an honest conversation.

Shift Two: Let Your Work Speak Before You Do

One of the hardest things for introverted entrepreneurs is the idea of constantly talking about themselves. It can feel self-indulgent, uncomfortable, or just exhausting. So don't lead with yourself. Lead with your work.

What do I mean? Share what you know. Write a post about a problem your ideal client has — not to sell, but to help. Answer a question in a community or forum. Share a perspective that's genuinely useful. Create something small that demonstrates your thinking.

This is where introverts often thrive, because depth of thinking is something introverts tend to have in abundance. A single, thoughtful piece of writing can do more to attract the right client than a hundred promotional posts. It signals expertise. It creates trust before you've even spoken. And it lets people come to you — which, for many introverts, is a much more comfortable dynamic than the constant outreach cycle.

A note on content

You don't have to post every day. You don't have to be on every platform. Pick one place where your ideal clients spend time and show up there consistently — even if consistently means once a week. Depth over frequency. Quality over volume.

✦

Shift Three: Make Asking Easy — for Them and for You

Here's the piece that most introverted entrepreneurs skip: you have to make it easy for interested people to take the next step with you. Not pushy. Just clear.

This means having a simple way for someone to express interest — a link to book a call, an email address you mention explicitly, a clear statement of who you work with and how. Because even when someone is genuinely interested in working with you, if the path forward is unclear, they'll hesitate. And you'll interpret their hesitation as rejection and pull further back.

The most introvert-friendly version of this: stop thinking of the sales conversation as a pitch and start thinking of it as a conversation to find out if you're a good fit. You're not convincing anyone of anything. You're exploring together. That reframe alone changes the energy you bring to it — and people feel that.

"You don't need to convince anyone. You need to find the people who are already looking for exactly what you offer — and make sure they can find you."

The Real Secret: Rooted, Not Loud

There's a reason I used the word "rooted" in the title of this post. Because what actually attracts clients — especially for coaches, consultants, and service providers — isn't volume or visibility. It's groundedness.

When you know what you do, who you help, and why it matters — when you can talk about your work with calm certainty instead of apologetic hedging — people feel it. And they trust it. You don't need to be the loudest person in the room. You need to be the most clear.

Clarity is magnetic. Certainty is magnetic. And the good news is that both are things you can cultivate — regardless of how many Instagram posts you make.

The three shifts in summary

  • Stop networking, start connecting. One genuine conversation beats fifty hollow pitches. Reach back into your real relationships first.
  • Let your work speak before you do. Share what you know. Demonstrate your thinking. Create trust before the conversation happens.
  • Make the next step clear. You're not selling — you're making it easy for the right person to say yes to exploring a fit.
✦

Your first client is much closer than it feels. They're likely already in your orbit — someone who's been watching, thinking, waiting for the right moment to reach out. Your job isn't to chase them. Your job is to make sure they know you're there, that you're clear on who you help, and that reaching out feels safe and easy for them.

That's not hustling. That's just being a genuinely helpful person who has built something worth finding.

And that? You can do that as an introvert. In fact — you might be better at it than anyone.
