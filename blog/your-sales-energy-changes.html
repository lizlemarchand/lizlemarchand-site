Your Sales Energy Changes
When You Stop Managing
Everyone's Opinion

Most women don't hate selling. They hate what selling might make people think of them.

By Liz Lemarchand · June 27, 2026 · 8 min read

Most women who struggle with selling aren't actually afraid of selling.

They're afraid of what selling might make people think about them.

"Will they think I'm pushy?"

"Will they think I only care about money?"

"Will they still like me if I follow up?"

"Will they think I'm too much?"

So instead of selling, they manage.

They soften the offer until it barely sounds like an offer. They frame the price as optional. They add "no pressure" before anyone has raised a single objection. They wait for someone to ask instead of making the invitation themselves.

And that is exhausting.

Not because selling is hard. Because staying liked is a full-time job.

✦

The Real Weight Isn't the Sales Conversation. It's the Surveillance.

When your goal in a sales conversation is to stay liked, you stop being a woman with something valuable to offer. You become a woman monitoring every reaction, scanning for signs of disapproval, and adjusting your words in real time to make sure no one feels uncomfortable.

That's not a sales problem. That's a nervous system problem.

Your body is running a threat detection loop: Will they still like me? Am I too much? Did I push too hard? And it's burning through energy that was supposed to go toward communicating your value.

The offer gets buried. The price gets mumbled. The follow-up never gets sent.

Not because you don't believe in what you do. Because, in that moment, being liked feels more urgent than being clear.

✦

Why "Just Be More Confident" Doesn't Work

Most sales advice skips this entirely.

It tells you to be bolder. Louder. More assertive. To "lean in" and "own your worth" and stop shrinking.

But confidence that isn't rooted in something real doesn't hold. You can practice a script and still feel hollow delivering it. You can post the price and still immediately follow it with three paragraphs of justification. You can make the offer and still spend two days wondering if you came on too strong.

The confidence you need in sales isn't a performance. It's a settled sense of who you are and what you're offering.

And you cannot access that when you're spending your energy managing what other people think.

"When you stop making other people's opinion your full-time job, something shifts. The offer gets cleaner. The energy gets quieter. And the right people can finally feel it."

✦

What Opinion Management Actually Looks Like in Sales

It's worth naming these directly, because most women don't recognize them as a pattern. They just think they're being "polite" or "considerate."

Softening language that doesn't need softening. "This might not be for everyone, but..." "I don't know if this is something you're looking for, but..." These phrases read as hedging, not humility.

Pre-emptive apology. Adding "no pressure, obviously" before anyone has pushed back. You've answered an objection that hasn't been raised and trained the other person to hear your offer as low-confidence.

Price mumbling. Stating the investment quickly, quietly, or buried inside a paragraph of other information. The way you say the price is the first signal people get about how you feel about it.

Waiting instead of inviting. Telling yourself you don't want to be pushy, so you mention the offer once and never bring it up again. That's not respect. That's avoidance.

The over-explanation spiral. Listing seventeen reasons your offer is worth it before the other person has even had a chance to sit with it. Over-justification signals insecurity, not value.

✦

What Changes When You Stop

When you stop managing everyone's opinion, the sales conversation changes texture.

Not because you've become a different person. Because you're no longer trying to be every version of yourself at once — the one who sells and the one who is universally liked and the one who never makes anyone feel pressured and the one who stays small enough not to trigger anyone.

You just become the woman who knows what she offers, says it clearly, and lets people decide.

That's not pushy. That's respect. Respect for your work, and respect for the person you're talking to.

When you stop hedging, they can actually hear the offer.

When you stop mumbling the price, they can actually receive it.

When you stop pre-apologizing, they stop bracing for something to apologize for.

Sales gets quieter. Not because you're doing less, but because you're no longer doing two things at once.

✦

This Is What We Call Sales as Self-Leadership

There's a version of selling that doesn't feel like performing. It doesn't involve fake confidence, louder energy, or forcing yourself to "just get over it."

It involves getting honest about the real reason selling feels heavy.

Not because the process is wrong. Because the goal underneath the process is "please don't stop liking me."
