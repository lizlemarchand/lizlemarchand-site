Money Mindset

Why Asking for Money
Feels Like a Threat
(And What to Do About It)

It's not a sales problem. It's a nervous system problem — and once you understand that, everything changes.

By Liz Lemarchand · June 25, 2025 · 7 min read

You have something worth selling. You know it. And yet the moment someone asks, "How much do you charge?" — your stomach drops, your chest tightens, and you either blurt out a number that's half of what you meant to say, or you apologize for it before they can respond.

This isn't a confidence problem. It isn't a strategy problem. And it definitely isn't a "you just need to hustle harder" problem.

It's a threat response. And your body is doing exactly what it was designed to do.

✦

Your Nervous System Doesn't Know the Difference

Here's what's actually happening when you make an offer and feel that rush of dread: your brain is pattern-matching. It's scanning your history, your conditioning, your earliest lessons about money and worth and what happens when women ask for things — and it's flagging the situation as dangerous.

The amygdala — the part of your brain responsible for threat detection — doesn't distinguish between "a tiger is chasing me" and "I'm about to ask for $500 and someone might say no." To your nervous system, rejection is a survival threat. Being judged for wanting too much feels like exile. And exile, evolutionarily speaking, meant death.

So your body does what it always does when it perceives danger: it braces. Heart rate up. Palms damp. Voice a little higher than usual. The prefrontal cortex — your reasoning brain, the one that knows your price is fair — goes slightly offline. And what comes out instead is smaller, softer, more apologetic than you meant.

"You don't need to become pushier. You need to become more rooted in your own worth — so that asking for money stops feeling like a threat."

Where the Pattern Comes From

For most women, the discomfort around money and asking isn't random. It has roots. Deep ones.

Maybe you grew up watching the women in your family minimize what they needed so no one would be inconvenienced. Maybe you were taught — directly or through the air you breathed — that ambition was unattractive. That wanting a lot was greedy. That good women give more than they take.

Maybe you've been in situations where asking for more cost you something: a relationship, an approval, a sense of safety. And your nervous system catalogued all of it.

None of this is your fault. But it is your work to do.

What this conditioning often sounds like

  • "I don't want to seem greedy."
  • "What if they think I'm not worth it?"
  • "I should probably offer a discount — just to be safe."
  • "Maybe I'll wait until I have more experience before I charge that much."
  • "I feel bad charging people I know."
  • "What if they say no and it's awkward?"

Sound familiar? These aren't logical conclusions. They're old programs running in the background — and they will keep running until you do something to interrupt them.

✦

The Three Layers of the Money Block

In my work with women entrepreneurs, I've found that the fear of asking for money almost always lives on three levels simultaneously. Understanding all three is what makes lasting change possible.

1. The Nervous System Layer

This is the physical, bodily response. The tightening. The rush. The sudden urge to give a discount before they've even said anything. This is where somatic practices, breathwork, and regulation tools come in. Before you can change your beliefs, you need to be able to tolerate the discomfort of asking without going into full fight-or-flight. Your window of tolerance has to expand.

2. The Belief Layer

Underneath the physical response are the stories. "I'm not experienced enough." "People like me don't make that kind of money." "I'll lose the client if I charge what I actually want." These are NLP territory — limiting beliefs that can be identified, examined, and replaced. They feel like facts. They are not facts.

3. The Identity Layer

The deepest layer is who you believe yourself to be in relation to money. If you grew up identifying as "the girl who didn't have much," or if your sense of self is tied to being generous to a fault, or if some part of you believes that the price of being loved is staying small — that's an identity-level block. This is where the real transformation happens. Not in tactics. In self-concept.

✦

What Actually Helps

Here's the honest truth: you can memorize every sales script in existence and still fumble your prices if the nervous system piece isn't addressed. So let's start there.

Regulate before you make an offer

Before a sales conversation, give yourself five minutes. A walk. Box breathing. Shaking out your hands and feet. Whatever helps your body come down from anticipatory anxiety. You want your prefrontal cortex online when you're talking about money — not hijacked by fear.

Name the sensation, don't become it

When you feel the dread rise as you're about to say your price — notice it. "There it is. The contraction. The old pattern." Naming the sensation gives you just enough distance to not be completely run by it. You can feel the fear and say the number anyway. That gap — between the feeling and the action — is where your power lives.

Say your price. Then stop talking.

This is the hardest and most important skill. Most women undersell not because they charge too little, but because they immediately fill the silence after their price with justifications, apologies, and offers to discount. The silence after your price feels unbearable because your nervous system reads it as threat. Practice tolerating it. The client is processing. They don't need you to rescue them — or yourself.

Reconnect with the value, not the transaction

When money feels scary, we focus on what we're asking for. Shift to what you're offering. What changes for someone when they work with you? What burden lifts? What becomes possible? When you're rooted in the real value of your work, the price becomes secondary. You're not asking for money. You're offering a transformation — and naming what it costs.

"Selling from fear says: 'Please buy so I can feel okay.' Selling from worth says: 'I know what this is worth. I'd love to explore whether it's right for you.'"

This Is the Work No One Talks About

I've spent 25 years in sales and marketing. I've closed deals that would make most people's heads spin. And I still had to do this work. The rewiring. The nervous system regulation. The excavation of the beliefs that were running my behavior without my permission.

This isn't a women-only problem — but women carry particular layers of conditioning around money, worth, and visibility that have to be deliberately unraveled. It doesn't happen by accident. And it doesn't happen by just "believing in yourself" harder.

It happens when you learn to feel safe in your body while asking. When you recognize the old stories for what they are. When you build an identity that no longer requires you to stay small to stay loved.

That's the work. It's slower than learning a closing technique. But it's the work that actually lasts.

✦

If you recognized yourself anywhere in this post — the stomach drop, the apologetic pricing, the convincing yourself you're "not ready yet" — I want you to know something: that response is not evidence that you're bad at business. It's evidence that you're human, with a nervous system that's doing its job, running patterns that were built a long time ago.

And patterns can change.
